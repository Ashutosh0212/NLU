How natural or unnatural the chatbot feels:

The Reggy++ chatbot has a mixed feel when it comes to naturalness. On the positive
side, the flow of conversation is structured in a logical way: it greets you, asks
for your name, then birthday, then mood. This feels somewhat natural because it
mirrors how a person might get to know someone new. The responses to different
moods (happy, sad, tired, etc.) are varied and empathetic, which helps the
chatbot feel less robotic.

However, there are clear unnatural aspects. The chatbot is very rigid in its
expectations. It must receive information in a specific order and format. If you
try to say "I'm feeling tired and my birthday is 15-02-2000" in one message, it
will likely only catch one part or get confused. The chatbot also repeats the
same prompts when it doesn't understand, which can feel frustrating. For example,
when given an invalid date, it asks for the birthday again without acknowledging
what went wrong in a conversational way. The lack of memory beyond the current
session (name, age, mood) means it cannot refer back to earlier parts of the
conversation naturally.


Strengths of regex-based interaction:

The main strength of using regular expressions is simplicity and predictability.
There are no external libraries needed, and the logic is transparent. Once you
define a pattern, you know exactly what will match. This makes the chatbot easy
to debug and extend. For well-defined tasks like parsing dates in known formats
(dd-mm-yyyy, Month dd YYYY, etc.), regex works quite well. We can handle multiple
formats with a few patterns.

Another strength is typo tolerance. By using flexible patterns like "hap+y" or
"hapy|hapi|hppy" for "happy", we can catch common spelling mistakes without
complex fuzzy matching. The same approach works for mood words. Surname
extraction is trivial with regex—just split on whitespace and take the last
word. For a constrained domain, regex gives good coverage with minimal code.


Limitations and failure cases:

The biggest limitation is that regex cannot understand context or intent. If the
user says "I was born on 15th of February 2000", the chatbot might not extract
the date correctly because "15th" has letters. We would need more patterns to
handle "15th", "1st", "22nd" etc. Similarly, "my birthday is next month" or "I
turned 25 last week" cannot be handled at all.

Ambiguous dates are another problem. For "03-04-2000", is it March 4 or April 3?
The chatbot tries both and picks the first valid date, but this could be wrong
depending on the user's locale. There is no way to ask for clarification.

Mood detection fails when the user expresses mood indirectly. "I had a rough day"
or "everything is going wrong" would not match any mood pattern. We can only
handle explicit mood words. The typo patterns help but cannot cover all possible
misspellings.

The failure case in the log (Run 6) shows another issue: when the user gives an
invalid date and then says "bored", the chatbot still expects a date. It does not
recognize "bored" as a mood because the conversation state is stuck on the
birthday question. A more natural chatbot would be able to handle out-of-order
or mixed inputs.

Overall, regex-based chatbots work well for narrow, formulaic interactions but
struggle with the variety and flexibility of human conversation.
